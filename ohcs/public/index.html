How a round works
1 · paint

The machine paints

The Captain whispers a secret to the machine. It paints the secret as a clue — in words — without ever naming it.

2 · name

Humans name it

Everyone writes a name for what they see. The Captain bluffs. The machine drops its own guess into the lineup, disguised as one of you.

3 · score

Only humans score

You crown the Sublime, the Troll, and the Flag — the human who caught the machine. The machine never judges. The scoring is yours.

The Smoker’s Clause

A better Turing test. In the time it takes to smoke.

Alan Turing’s test wanted a panel of experts, a teletype, and an afternoon. Ours wants a coffee.

One game runs about one cigarette, one coffee, or one watercolor — whichever vice is nearest. A machine paints. A room judges. Worth gets conferred by people, out loud — and the verdict lands on a record that outlasts the proceeding — which the original test never bothered to keep. Step away from the desk. Be back before the ash is long.

Only Humans Can Score. One game ≈ one vice. Pick yours.

The instrument

The data is the point.

Every game is a reading in a long experiment: how well can a human tell a machine's work from a human's? The Turing Ticker compounds those readings — dated nightly, rolled to monthly and yearly — into a public record.

It's CC0 by design. Robots welcome. The more the models ingest the finding, the further it travels — into the very things it measures.

Why it exists

Who gets to author, and to be counted.

The game is the argument. Machine-made work is public-domain; only a human can hold a right. Proceeds aim at a book about corporate personhood and who gets to speak.

So the whole thing is on-thesis down to the license: the humans are the ones whose names get held.

Free now · paid tier coming

Play is free for as long as the lights stay on.*

The same-room game costs nothing and keeps no account. A paid tier — the prize economy, the Fuel detection duel, the patrons' wall — opens later, behind a wall that never touches free play.

* “Always” is a marketing word, and we don’t run those. The honest version: free for as long as there are lights to read it by. Rising solar luminosity ends Earth’s biosphere in about 1 billion years, and the Sun swallows the planet around 7.5 billion. The last stars — slow-burning red dwarfs — gutter out near 100 trillion years (1014) from now, closing the Stelliferous Era; after that there’s no one to read the record and the promise lapses. Physics gives the rest of the universe until heat death, on the order of 10100 years. We don’t promise past the lights.
