Fuel — man v machine

The main game asks if a human can tell the machine's work apart. Fuel runs it in reverse. You feed a specimen — a photo, a painting, an AI render — and Charlissian must call it: made by a human, or generated by a machine? You know the truth. It doesn't. Stump it and you score. Best of the lot wins the duel.

Every call is fuel: a labeled reading that feeds the record of how well a machine can tell human from machine — and how well you can fool it.

How this becomes fuel. In the live product, each row is anonymized into the corpus — specimen type, declared origin, the machine's call, the outcome, a timestamp — never the image itself. Aggregated nightly, it becomes a new board on the Turing Ticker: machine detection accuracy over time, and the human stump-rate against it. The duel powers the data; the data powers the argument.
The RoboCop Award. Each month, the human with the highest stump-rate — part curator, part machine-hunter — takes the RoboCop Award and a spot on the board. (Parked: needs the live monthly leaderboard. Name pending trademark clearance.)
Honesty: the verdict comes from a general vision model, not a provenance detector — neither humans nor machines reliably tell AI from human work, which is exactly why the data is worth keeping. Declared origin is your word, unverified; the real provenance standard is C2PA / Content Credentials, parked for the build. Images are sent to the model to be judged and are not stored by this game. Fuel is a game and an experiment, never proof about a real work or person.
