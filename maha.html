MAHA: Make America Healthy Again, Graded

"Here's actually how" starts by refusing the team sport. MAHA names a real problem and then trips over its own evidence. So this grades it claim by claim — what the science backs, what it doesn't, and the awkward part where the movement's own flagship report broke the first rule of this whole site: it faked its citations.

What it is

"Make America Healthy Again" (MAHA) is the health movement led by HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. Its MAHA Commission released a 78-page report in May 2025 on childhood chronic disease, followed by a ~120-initiative strategy in September 2025. The stated targets: ultra-processed foods, chemical exposures, over-prescription, and lifestyle. Some of that is solid ground. Some of it isn't. The honest move is to separate them.

The traps (both directions)

Trap 1 — total dismissal. "MAHA is all nonsense" throws out a correct, important point: American chronic disease and the ultra-processed diet are a real crisis. The diagnosis isn't the problem.
Trap 2 — total embrace. "They're finally telling the truth" launders the debunked claims (vaccines, fluoride) on the strength of the good ones. A movement can be right about lunch and wrong about vaccines in the same breath.
Trap 3 — the fabricated-citation tell. This is the big one for this site: a report that cites studies that don't exist fails Rule One — never fake a record (Art. I). You can share a goal and still refuse the forgery used to sell it.

"Here's actually how" — the evidence-backed version

If the goal is genuinely a healthier country, the levers with the strongest evidence are unglamorous: cut ultra-processed foods, make whole food affordable and accessible (the commons again — pools, parks, groceries), keep childhood vaccination high, keep fluoride at recommended levels, move more, and fund prevention. You can pursue every good MAHA instinct and keep the parts of public health that already saved millions. The slogan isn't the problem; the forged evidence and the debunked science are.
