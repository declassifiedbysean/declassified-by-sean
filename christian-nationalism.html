Christian Nationalism: Read It Yourself

A loaded term that gets thrown both as a badge and as an insult. So the lesson is the usual one: define it precisely, separate the political ideology from the faith, see how scholars actually measure it — and notice that some of its sharpest critics are devout Christians. Read the sources; don't let either side define it for you.

First, the distinction that does all the work

Christian nationalism is not the same thing as being a Christian, being a Christian in politics, or believing your faith should shape how you vote. Scholars define it as a political and cultural ideology that seeks to fuse American national identity with a particular Christian identity and to privilege that Christianity in the nation's laws, symbols, and public life. The faith is a religion; the nationalism is a claim about who the country belongs to. Conflating the two is the single most common error in every direction.

The two honest sides

AWhat supporters say. That the nation was founded on Christian principles, that public life has been stripped of a moral foundation, and that restoring an explicitly Christian framework would be a return, not an imposition. Many who hold "Christian nation" views would not call themselves "nationalists" at all.
BWhat critics say — including many Christians. That fusing church and state violates the Establishment Clause and the religious liberty that protects all faiths (and none); that it can shade into ethno-nationalism; and that, theologically, making an idol of the nation betrays a Gospel about a kingdom "not of this world." The Baptist tradition in particular has historically championed church–state separation.

The traps (in both directions)

Trap 1 — the smear. Using "Christian nationalist" to mean "any Christian who votes their conscience." That's a slur, not a definition, and it flattens millions of ordinary believers.
Trap 2 — the dodge. Insisting the term is "meaningless" to avoid the documented ideology that scholars do, in fact, define and measure. It has a referent.
Trap 3 — the conflation. Treating "Christianity," "the Religious Right," and "Christian nationalism" as one blob. They overlap and they differ; precision is the whole job.

The "real mission" question — handled fairly

A great many Christians argue that the faith's actual assignment is the opposite of seizing state power: care for the orphan and the widow (James 1:27), show no favoritism (James 2:1), love the neighbor and the stranger, wash feet. That's a theological argument made by believers against nationalism — and it's a legitimate, sourced position in this debate, not DECLASSIFIED putting words in anyone's mouth. Whether the "real mission" is national restoration or humble service is precisely the argument; this page just makes sure you can see both claims clearly and check them.
