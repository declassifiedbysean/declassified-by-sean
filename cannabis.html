Legalize It: The Alcohol Model, Graded

The pitch is simple and it's mostly right: legalize cannabis at 21+, regulate it, and sin-tax it like alcohol. So this grades it against the record — what the model actually delivers, and the one place it reliably blows up. Spoiler: the framework works; the dials are the whole argument.

What it is

The "alcohol model" for cannabis: an age gate at 21, a state-licensed retail market, lab-tested product, and an excise ("sin") tax — the same playbook the country already runs for liquor. It is no longer hypothetical. As of 2026, 24 states and Washington, D.C. have legalized adult-use cannabis; Colorado and Washington opened the first legal markets back in 2014. There's a decade of receipts now. The honest move is to read them.

The claims, graded against the record

ClaimVerdictWhy (sourced)
"Regulate-and-tax raises real public money."TRUEAdult-use states collected ~$4.4B in 2025 alone and $29B+ since 2014. The revenue is real and recurring.
"A 21+ age gate, like alcohol, is the right line."DEFENSIBLEIt mirrors the existing, broadly-accepted alcohol standard and is the line every legal state adopted. Reasonable; still debated at the edges (medical exceptions, etc.).
"Legal, tested product beats an unregulated one."TRUEIllicit product is untested for pesticides, mold, and contaminants; a regulated market puts testing and labeling between the consumer and the harm.
"Legalization by itself kills the black market."MISLEADINGNot automatically. In California — one of the oldest markets — an estimated ~60% of sales still happen illicitly, and illegal cultivation outpaces the legal market by nearly 10×.
"You can tax it as high as you want — it's a cash cow."FALSECalifornia's cannabis tax reaches ~78% of wholesale value (vs. 8.4% for alcohol, 29.5% for tobacco). Over-tax it and consumers walk to the cheaper illicit seller — rebuilding the market you were replacing.

The traps (both directions)

Trap 1 — prohibition nostalgia. "Just keep it illegal" ignores the $29B+ in revenue, the testing safety floor, and the enormous human cost of criminalization. The status quo had a price too; it just didn't show up on a tax form.
Trap 2 — legalize-then-gouge. The seductive mistake: treat legal weed as a bottomless ATM and tax it at 4–9× the alcohol rate. California did, and handed ~60% of the market back to the people it legalized to put out of business. The tax that's too high funds the cartel, not the classroom.
Trap 3 — the access desert. Nearly 60% of California cities and counties ban dispensaries. Legal on paper, unavailable in practice — and an unavailable legal market is just a subsidy for the illegal one. Legalization without access is theater.

"Here's actually how" — the version the record supports

The model genuinely works; the failures are tuning errors, not proof the engine's broken. The evidence-backed dial settings: 21+ age gate; tax it like alcohol, not like a sin you're punishing (single digits to the ~low-30s, nowhere near 78%); broad retail access so the legal product is actually reachable; mandatory lab testing; and route the revenue to treatment, education, and the communities prohibition hit hardest. Do that and the legal market displaces the illicit one. Gouge it and you've just run a very expensive experiment in re-creating your dealer.
